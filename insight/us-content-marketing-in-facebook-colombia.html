How does content marketing work in a 150,000+ online community?

Virginia Stein

In this article ❧ How does content marketing work? How appeals to the wants and needs of consumers? How is it visually? Does it work the same with both men and women? Is it more effective in certain segments?

The power of Facebook is not limited to traditional advertising and marketing. Many brands fish with dynamite in that social network, but there are also many brands that publish --or encourage the publication of-- contents that subtly position their products, without appearing to be selling.

Beyond recipes and Best Sellers, how does content marketing really work. Let's see an specific case: an online community on Facebook, whose target are matured, middle class men, to whom digital camera gear is a must have (since they are amateur digital photographers)

Target segment: mature men with money ... who consume content on Facebook

Men of Generation X (between 36 to 55 years old), with purchasing power, have a growing interest in hobbies like photography. If we focus on the segments with professional and postgraduate education, which have developed long trajectories in companies, or in the exercise of professions such as medicine, we find that the majority have a medium income and that are inclined to get involved in a hobby, if it promises considerable relief from work stress or a fantasy realization . In Colombia, less than 58% of middle and hight income adult, men and women, are inclined to gambling (41 % of these , however, do that occasionally); only 15% associates it with get out of the routine.

At the opposite extreme we have even lower numbers. When they pass the 40-year-old barrier, Colombians go less to the movies ( 28.6% in the segment from 41 to 64 years old , against 49% in the segment from 26 to 40 years). The same thing happens with reading but in an attenuated way (40% vs. 49%, respectively).

It is clear that gambling is very risky , and that movies or books are too safe. Hard hobbies are thus an alternative intermediate; and in the age of the Internet the best place to extract insights about hobbies are communities online. The reasons are several, but they are summarized in one: free and dense exchange of information. All the tools Internet (forums, image, video, chats, downloads, anonymity) make it easy for those who enter the world of a hobby stay there for a long time.

Flickr and The Brothers Brick are excellent examples. The first was for years the best website for photography hobbyists (and for market photographic products); and the second the site number one for the adult lego fan segment. Does it happen in Colombia? Are there colossal online communities, focused on a product segment, from which insights can be extracted? Yes, and the rest of this text is dedicated to one of them.

Generation X in Marketing

In market research Generation X is the people who were born in the mid of the 1960s and early during the 1980s. It is the generation immediately after the Baby Boom generation (those born between 1946 and 1964), and the one immediately preceding the Millenials (1981 to 2000). According to WARC Generation X can be characterized with the next insights:

  1. They resemble Millennials in media consumption.
  2. They have a pessimistic view of banks
  3. They are obsessed with working and are not very interested in family.
  4. They don't feel reflected in marketing.
  5. Advertising has failed to portray its consumer impulses

An online community that prohibits selling ... and allows selling

Let's Learn Photography was created on Facebook in 2017 and It has 152,226 members. For comparison, the Brothers-brick Facebook page has twice, but it is older. When visiting the Information of the community the first thing that jumps out is the abundance of rules. They are a total of 24 prohibitions and there is even one that is repeated twice: "no nudity". They are also not allowed links nor sales. However, in this case the interpretation suggests that the prohibitions precisely point out practices that are frequent in the group. In other words, although they do not post nude, erotic content is posted constantly and, although people can't sell, all the time people sell.

The following text contains an in-depth interview with one of the members of the community. Roberto He is 42 years old, manager of a retail branch , and practices photography as a hobby for five years (he has taken pictures long before). In its profile three things highlight: his postgraduate education, that he has no children and that he wants to use the photography for rest from work stress . Is member of the community of Let's learn photography 1 year ago and six months ago he bought his first DSLR camera (a big camera of interchangeable lenses).

From shopping center marketing to content marketing

I bought my first camera at Salitre Plaza . My wife wanted that we had something decent to take pictures of our dogs . She did not like the idea that we took photos with a Nokia. I think it was because her dad was a journalist and she had a dark room in her house; a big house in Nice.

What brand it was. It was a Sony Mavica, a weird camera. I bought it at Fotojapon. Then I realized two stuff. One, that when I bought it that was already an outdated technology, and another that the price I had paid was absurd, no only because it was obsolete but because its price in dollars was much more low-.

One day, I don't know why [my italics], I started searching on the internet for forums about cameras. I searched in English, because I assumed that in Spanish I wasn't going to find anything valuable, technically speaking. I found a site called Photonet , an American page where people talked about a bunch of things that I had hardly thought existed: lenses, sensors, film, lights, studios. "And they posted Photos. And I spent the whole weekend looking at photos and reading, just for the pleasure of seeing photos and reading. On Monday, when I was at work, I couldn't stop, it was like addiction to pornography without pornography.

It didn't take more than two weeks to want to move to a new level. I ran away from the office one afternoon; sales were going Well, the bosses were abroad at a congress; the quiet office environment. I sold my outdated camera and bought a Canon Rebel.

I'm not sorry to say that I was afraid to go outside with a Rebel. [Here Roberto narrates widely bad experiences with security in Bogotá.] (...). Anyone weekend tried to recover from various hectic weeks in the job. The company had resolved to change the logo, change the website, and they had also fired various middle managers and base personnel. I don't want to talk about that anymore. On Saturday I realized on another page that I had met in Photonet, Dpreview , that the lens my Rebel came with was ordinary, plastic, not so sharp, as the professional lenses, the L , they were much better. That day I went out to take photos and the camera hit the door. The Lens autofocus no longer worked. On Sunday I was to San Andresito and bought an used L lens, for another million and a half.

(...) I took photos for fun. I don't remember the last time I took out that camera. Maybe it was on the my marriage sister in law. My brother-in-law, who is a doctor, took out his Nikon, and insisted that it was faster than my Canon. I showed him it didn't, and that also mine had higher resolution.
