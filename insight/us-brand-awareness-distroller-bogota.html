The value proposition of Distroller for Bogotá

Paulo César León Palacios

In this article ❧ Distroller - the Mexican toy brand - has arrived in Colombia. Its main strength is the attraction that many Colombians feel for Mexican culture. But that is also its main weakness: What would happen if distroller fails to sufficiently translate the cultural codes on which its products are based?

Mattel, Distroller, "México"

Mexico as brand

Mexico is a brand, and this is an asset to any other Mexican brand that wants to position itself abroad. This applies even to the United States, a market that includes some xenophobia (and an ambivalent attraction for) as an spicy obstacle; and it applies to Colombia, a country where it is not difficult to find various manifestations of this taste for "the Mexican."

If one had to choose one of the causes of Mexico's success as a brand in Colombia, the influence of television might be chosen. The most obvious example is El chavo and Televisa's novels, which have been voraciously watched since the 1980s. Nowadays, El chavo is still part of the channels programming on some television channels and is almost as important as Acapulco Shore.

If a second cause had to be identified (business decisions must have sufficient knowledge of the context) this would be Mexican tourism marketing. Mexico has been able to sell itself as an exotic and magical destination: pyramids, food and the style of La Rioja have been fundamental parts of it. In the case of Colombia, we should add the Mariachi - which seems to be used more in Bogotá than in Mexico City.

Enough context to take for granted the decision to bring Distroller to Colombia.

Insight

Distroller : "Mexico" is a brand, and is an asset for any Mexican brand that wants to position itself abroad. The only difficulty - and it is not complicated to overcome it - is to properly translate the cultural codes on which Mexican marketing outside Mexico is based.

Distroller Value Proposition

Distroller is a toy brand - for girls (and kul boys?). The value proposition is to provide an unrealistic, fantastic experience of the classic line of dolls that simulate motherhood, with dresses, accessories and others.

Now, Distroller plays with the following specific value propositions:

  • "Design 102 percent Mexican": it works, among other things, because Mexican painting and crafts (including the the famous Mexican rose ) enjoy perceived quality (Aaker) and even brand loyalty in cities like New York.
  • "Distroller does not educate, he only gives his humble opinion." This is intelligent in a cultural context in which passionate positions and emotional overflows are common, from high politics to the anonymity of social networks. Distroller, on the other hand, does not sell correct ideas , rather it makes money with subtle social criticism. Later we continue with this point.
  • Freedom, creativity vindication of diversity: Distroller's products are explicitly aimed at girls of diverse ethnic backgrounds, who are part of various types of families.

Challenges the brand faces

The segment of toys for girls is much more dynamic than that of boys (for better and for worse). This is because cultural representations about femininity and childhood are in a severe process of mutation. Let's see:

  • Ideas about motherhood are increasingly ambiguous and demanding.
  • The rising educational levels in the female population have created a more picky demand in the middle classes.
  • The impact of feminist discourse (of which brands like Mattel have been appropriated).
  • Lost of value on the shelves of the stores (it is increasingly common to see greater discounts on the toy lines "for girls"

Design evolution

The fundamental change is in the expressiveness of the design. In 2009 Chamoy had hypnotic pupils, no movement, and too elementary eyelashes. The hair solution was smart (lines like curls) but not glamorous. The makeup was flat (for example on the cheeks), while the accessory on the forehead ("balaca") received more attention.

In 2019 we have the same solution: a drawing with a large head (standard of children's drawing), the same colors, the same accessories. But at the same time we have something new: a character that projects emotions, which in 2009 did not happen. He does it with hands (in 2009 hidden), eyes, mouth and leg movement (see Image 1) .

Insight

Distroller : One of the fundamental changes in the brand image is the expressiveness in the design (another, no doubt, is the diversity in the product portfolio). That strategy has created value for the brand. On the other hand, one of the great challenges is to translate some of the cultural codes, successfully used in Mexico and Spain, to not depend exclusively on the popularity of Mexican folklore abroad.

Relationship with customers

Distroller projects "good ondez". He does it consciously. Aware that it is not the same to position a brand today than 59 years ago, when Barbie was born (the first Barbie was marketed in 1959). The barbies projected beauty, a white, thin, green-eyed, suburbial beauty, which operated in a market ruled by racist and traditional values ​​(the family based on the provider husband, was one of the main norms). Nowadays, the market demands things like good education and politeness. Distroller's good spirit is expressed in his language, full of invented words (virgencita plis, mexican pagüer, amiguis foreber), taken from the Latino speech ; or at least the representation of them that has the Distroller marketing.

However, Distroller's distribution channels have changed, or, rather, have expanded. Before the brand distributed in its Mexican stores, but now distributes in several South American countries, Spain and the United States. However, Distroller persists in the same language codes, relying exclusively on the Mexican charism of the brand.

What if a Colombia or Spanish, buyer cannot even pronounce -even understand- the word "Neonatitlan"? What would happen if it is not understood that these are multiple allusions at the same time (Tenochtitlan, capital of the Aztec empire + neonates + sarcasm-marketing)? What if the names of the characters are confused with words that buyers in Colombia associate with other things (stuff that would be called “albur” in Mexico)? How do buyers receive Distroller's Mexican branding, compared to Mattel's Mexican branding? (see image 4) What do they think of their prices?
