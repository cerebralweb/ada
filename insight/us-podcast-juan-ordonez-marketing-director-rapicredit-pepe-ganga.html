A hierarchical company takes a long time to make decisions

Paulo César León Palacios
Listen

In this article ❧ Episode 2 of SmallData -Hexis' podcast- is dedicated to chat with Juan Pablo Ordonez, Marketing Director for brands like Pepe Ganga and Dafiti.

Traditional companies vs startups

The first strong point in the conversation was the comparison made by Juan Pablo about the differences between a company like Pepe Ganga and a digital startup like Dafiti. The former is very "traditional" compared to a digital company. Pablo was impressed, for example, by the enormous attention given in Pepe Ganga to the use of uniforms and badges, while at the same time there were difficulties in understanding issues such as the advent of electronic commerce.

When I was in Dafiti ... the clothes ... I dressed normal (...) and then come to Pepe Ganga and find all the uniformed auxiliaries (...) is a very big picture of the company, a company much more hierarchical, that it takes a long time to make decisions

In this comparative context, the issue of management emerged and how rational is the information on which decisions are based:

In Dafiti there was a lot of analysis of numbers, one could see statistics everywhere and in Pepe Ganga not so much (...)

Startups: better not from scratch

Juan and I continue talking about startups. Perhaps the most interesting point was when Juan Pablo gave us an insight about the starting point of these ventures: startups that are applying a previously tested model will normally have an advantage over those that start literally from scratch.

Startups, Pablo explains, tend to focus on gaining a fast market, even if it is not profitable, while more conventional companies focus more on obtaining immediate returns. This is because startup investors do not usually expect dividends but rather the appreciation of the part of the company they have bought.

Market research

I asked Pablo if market research was useful for ecommerce based companies. He thinks so, especially in two areas: in the interpretation of existing data and in the production of quality qualitative data.

We live in an abundance of data but we don't know what to do with it (...)
