Interview with Carlos Gutierrez, Technology Colombian investor

Paulo César León Palacios
Listen

In this article ❧ Episode 3 of SmallData - Hexis' podcast - is dedicated to conversing with Carlos Gutierrez,tech investor and co-founder of INVX, a venture capital firm that has participated in startups like Tpaga and Rappi.

Carlos studied business administration at the Universidad de los Andes and has a Master in digital business from the University of Barcelona. Currently, he works as an entrepreneur at INVX; but before it was part of Wayra Colombia.

Late ... but interesting

The appointment was at 5pm, but I connected 25 minutes late. My interviewee, however, greets me with kindness and excuses me without further explanation; throughout the interview I understood that this flexibility was not accidental, but that my guest knew what it means to be patient (he is an investor) and he is also a fine analyst.

In the interview, Carlos tells how he got into the world of Venture Capital and why he was attracted by technology as an investment niche. We also spoke extensively about contemporary concepts such as Fintech, about angel investors and venture capital in technology startups.

The disruption of the Fintechs

Technology and banks have always been together, but:

The idea is to leave behind the traditional part of banking, all that heavy structure that has, procedures, and the idea that everything has to be physical, offices (...) through the Internet of things, from the same smartphones you can give a new face to this sector (...) Obviously, it is an advantage in some towns that are far from banks or that have direct access to a Baloto or Efecty.

Carlos believes that the new technologies in the hands of the Fintechs will allow democratization and make banking more friendly; I adhere to the second advantage convinced. Without a doubt, it makes life easier for everyone so that a person can download an app and pay for public services or whatever.

Learning helps to undertake

When asked if his experience as an employee helped him during his time as an entrepreneur, Carlos was clear: being employed at various companies allowed him to have a 360-degree view. Thus, he learned what investors are like, what they are looking for and how to talk to them.

One should not speak the same to an angel investor, than to a corporate VC or a traditional investor, or an impact fund. Each one is focused on different things.

An angel tends to focus more on the team, adds Carlos. They usually know the subject and have previously invested in. For an impact investor, the most important thing is that this impact is significant and measurable. Venture Capital, on the other hand, are seeking to achieve good returns for their investors. Finally, corporate Venture Capitals are primarily focused on the "core", they are not only looking for financial return, but something innovative that adds value to their organization.

What return margin do investors usually expect?

It all depends on the investment volumes, the stage in which one is entering (...) and the stage in which one is leaving. This is a game between risks and return (...) If one as an angel is putting $ 10,000, he does not have a very large risk but he does have a high possibility of valuation. So an angel can invest in 10 or 20 companies; what a fund does is invest in about 10 companies; Furthermore, they are investing in a stage of less risk and, therefore, of less value (...) they expect [therefore] less return than an angel.

How do you talk to an investor

The investing angels want to participate, Carlos explains to me. The reason is that they usually know the investment area in depth. The entrepreneur must therefore be open to teamwork with the investor. The other issue is trust: according to Carlos, in the long term the person ends up being more important, and the relationships, than the idea itself; especially in little innovative ecosystems.

Are there differences between Latin American and American investors?

Failure is not punished in the United States. [In fact] Many times people want to know about failures, to see what has been learned (...). In the Colombian and Latin American culture, failure is still a bit punished, especially in angel investors, because in Venture Capital they are more sophisticated (...) there are still many people who made their money in the traditional sector or as employees, so it costs them kinda understand the sector [of tech startups]

One can see that the business life cycle was previously 60 years or more, and that the life cycle has been shortened much faster due to technology. Before an industry could last 10 20 years without changes, today that means being the last in the industry. This is not new, Carlos concludes, rather it has accelerated.
