When I turned 39 my wife wanted to give me a shirt. That weekend we went to a Hugo Boss store in Bogotá. When we entered the premises she left the a young man in his twenties passed, thin, well-groomed and wearing a suit to measure. What the seller did reminded me that buying is more than buy. First he analyzed my shoes (a good observer always starts with the shoes), then tried to determine what brand of pants he wore, noticed if you wore an expensive watch (no); finally, he saw my shirt. But actually it was not the last: all the time I would be going back and forth on that information to interpret how to serve me. Excellent: my seller was acting exactly how it should act.

I also had to act as a buyer. I entered the premises as if no it was the first time I entered - and bought - something of the brand (point to me). I entered accompanied by a foreigner (point for me, in a culture of consumption that still overvalues ​​the presence of the foreigner), my shoes they were a little worn but not old, and they were quite informal (minus a period), but they were from the latest adidas collection (plus one); while the salesman observed me I spoke and my way of speaking transmitted something: that I am a "cultured" person and an experienced buyer (two points for me).

To buy is to place an expectation in the other

Perhaps without being very aware of it, the seller was listening my way to speak, so that I could enrich that quick interpretation of myself clothing. What use was it? Much, then, almost without intending to was able to decide "the right words" to reinforce his sales strategy. Let's reverse the analysis for a moment to see to what extent the way we talk influences a sale. If the seller he would have spoken to me as "nero" what would have been my reaction: at least one sly smile.

- Hi, I want to see a white long-sleeved shirt for men.

- Armholes.

I exaggerate. Saying "desire" or "male" would have been aspirational or anachronistic, and that would have devalued me as a buyer of Hugo Boss. Whatever, if my seller had said sisas, coriander, simcard, cindirella, simón, or any of the other popular variations of a yes I would have felt disappointed; A mere word would have introduced too much noise.

What is buying?

Buying is more than choosing something and paying for it. Buying is a social act: it involves our relationships with others, emotions, memories and previous learning. My visit to Hugo Boss's place felt like something far from emotions like anger, fear or sadness and very close to feelings like love. Five years ago, on a fresh, open sky morning, Carmen and I got married in a Mexican hotel: I was wearing a full Hugo Boss outfit. In my buyer's memory that was important, that association between a happy event and the use of a brand.

In marketing, people usually talk about the 80/20 rule. Although this refers to middle class and consumer markets like the North American I think that also It applies to specific segments and niches in a city like Bogotá. The 80/20 rule says that 20% of a brand's customers buy 80% of your products . Maybe it's an exaggeration or something that shouldn't be taken literally but there are cases where the ratio is even more Low: 1.2% of Budweiser customers in the US account for 80% of your sales.

This little gift might help to grok what buy is.

Merchandise has a powerful influence on the people who buy it. Shopping is a powerful act of communication between a person and an object. Most pieces of advertising that we see have little relation to the real qualities of the products: and it works. We buy for what we associate with the purchased object (remember that it was my wife who wanted to give me the shirt), because we need to express what we are, or what we want to be, through it (for example, I'm a husband, Hugo Boss purchase, like a mantra). Although the merchandise has physical qualities that we certainly look at (I know a Hugo Boss shirt is made to last and look good during a decade or more) those qualities function as a vehicle or a material support of the meaning of the purchase (my wife buys me the shirt because she loves me).

Purchases and hormones

Sometimes shopping makes me happy. The purchase therefore becomes something special in my life: buying helps to endorse who we are and what we want to be . It is almost the same, but not the same. If you buy becomes particularly important for a certain segment of consumers it may be that that special act of buying has become painfully special. That pain usually arises because the distance between what consumers are and what they want to be is great; and if such a distance becomes large enough the pain turns into frustration and resentment. When people can't buy what they think they are they become people who protest . Is there any doubt that Chile, Colombia, Argentina or Brazil would see less animosity in the streets of their cities if they were societies with fully established middle classes?

The satisfaction in the purchase

Satisfaction is difficult to study. Determine if a person really stayed Satisfied requires approaches from various perspectives. A couple of days ago I manage to solve something at a local bank. The experience was, let's say, Really good. Hours later I received an email with a title somewhat Orwellian: "We know you were at our branch." They wanted me to fill a satisfaction survey!

    Surveys and their destinations

    I wondered, then, what to do. I said to myself:

  • Option 1: I interrupt the reading of my other emails. I read the various lines from "email marketing" (there are still people who pay Mailchimp for that). I make click on the button. I wait for your page to load. While the charge ends imagined me accepting one of the consumer credits that automated calls offer me weekly. I carefully fill in each question (Plato - I remembered - believed that the entire life of a philosopher would be dedicated to the solution of one single question.). In conclusion: no, the option 1 was not an option.
  • Option 2: fill out the survey quickly. Consequences: poor quality data and guilty customer for supplying false information (Do you want people to associate your brand with those emotions?).
  • Option 3: I don't fill out the survey (my choice). Consequences: bad data quality and annoyed customer.

Hugo Boss never sent out surveys. They did not need it, as follows from the first paragraphs of this chronicle of consumption. (I wonder if anyone in the BBVA's marketing department buys shirts at Hugo Boss.) Some brands approach to satisfaction in a more realistic way. Penney - the American retailer - send researchers to observe and come along with 50 women during several days. They wanted to know what their lives were, on a daily basis, and how they used products that the brand could offer in its stores, which meant observe how they bought, how they ate, what they cooked, etc.

Some economists believe that satisfaction is inversely proportional to the frequency with which something is consumed. When my wife and I go to a mall we walk little and slowly. The reason is that we only go to buy.
