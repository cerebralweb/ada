How to manage your brand during a social crisis?

Paulo César León Palacios

In this article ❧ The consumer feels a great desire to be interpreted: the crisis is perfect to demonstrate the fervor of brands for their well-being.

Marketing directors: think twice before cutting the market research and advertising budget during the times of crisis we are living. Remember, first of all, that consumer goods (the core of retail) are considered recession proof, provided that the relationship between context (crisis), price and quality is well managed. Second, remember that a crisis also creates business opportunities. And most importantly, remember that "the user," "the customer," "the consumer," is ultimately a human being who feels a great desire to be interpreted: The crisis is the most favorable situation to prove your loyalty for this premise. They, the buyers, will know how to recognize this and will appreciate it ... buying ... or helping the brand to grow through good vibes sent in the word of mouth of everyday life.

Marketing directors in countries going through crisis

What a crisis is?

A crisis can be a recession, that is, a period of time of several months in which negative numbers are presented in general indicators of the economy. Sometimes recessions are cyclical, that is, they occur every certain number of years (usually every 10) as a result of a stagnation of demand, or due to the impact of structural phenomena, for example, the growing trade deficit in the United States in front of China.

However, there may not be a recession and still there will be a crisis. That is the case of social unrest seen in recent years in countries such as Brazil, Chile, Argentina and, now Colombia. In these cases, curiously, the social conflict is facilitated by previous cycles of moderate economic growth (Colombia, unlike other Latin American countries, tends to grow steadily - even when the others do not grow - but always in a very moderate way).

How does this last form of social crisis work? Economic growth allows consumption expectations to grow and education coverage to increase. When these expectations (pension, stable employment, housing, etc.) are not fulfilled, citizens who are dissatisfied and with more critical ideas (the product of education) take the streets, and the crisis arises, from nowhere.

Why are crises relevant to brands?

  1. They affect the demand for some goods
  2. Brands are public referents of identity
  3. People always expect their symbolic referents to shed light on problems. Brands are important references such as religion or politics.
  4. During crises, collective thinking is more prone to change the meaning of things: things like brands, which can be placed on a pedestal, or repudiated, overnight; a political party is also a brand

Budget reductions do not work in the long term

Saving is almost always prudent (sometimes spending a lot is also prudent); cutting expenses is inevitable when a crash plan is executed. However, in long periods of time the lack of marketing and advertising weakens the health of the brand and market share. Nick Parsons argued that sometimes it is worth going against what the masses usually think about crises. He studied several cases in which advertisers increased, maintained or reduced their advertising budgets. In all cases, there was a decrease in short-term ROI. However, those who maintained or increased advertising and research spending gained market share by at least half a percentage point once the crisis ended.

Another example: in the midst of theglobal recession of 2008,Procter & Gamble and Unilever decided to keep spending on marketing, advertising and market research. These companies understood that the recession was an opportunity to gain market share at the expense of the weakest companies, which chose to reduce expenses in these areas.

    In a pre-crisis state brands must:

  • Tailor its strategy for each platform and channel and avoid a unique approach
  • Continue investing in a highly distinctive brand, with its own visual identity based on a color palette, product photos, logos and aesthetic and textual features that create distinction
  • avoid confusing the brand with performance metrics

Before a recession comes, brands must think of strategies on how to tackle the problem. But that can be done creatively. Bryan Mattimore, co-founder of Growth Engine Innovation Agency, leads clients to what he calls "wargame sessions" to prepare them for crises. The roles that players assume during the workshops are not only direct competitors, but also companies that could become new rivals. The goal is to anticipate the worst scenarios and create strategies to deal with them.

Information is power: be recursive

Remember that information is a scarce, valuable, spared resource. Information is the best tool to plan and make difficult decisions. Try to understand the value that consumers give the brand so that it is in a better position when creating strategies and innovations; use previous market studies, metrics and qualitative lightning studies. Have suppliers of different profiles, in case you need to consider low budgets in advertising, marketing and market research. Try to imagine all the scenarios and create strategies to deal with them. Continually review sales reports to learn about consumer trends. Organize meetings between manufacturers and retail customers to share ideas about new products, promotions and marketing campaigns: create value for others, without always expecting direct retribution. Think about how your brand can help relieve consumer anxiety in general. Create a butterfly effect.

This little gift can help.

Tune in to the consumer: his needs are the true "market needs"

"Be sensitive to your clients' difficulties," says David Reibstein, a marketing professor at the Wharton School of the University of Pennsylvania. "If you identify with them, they will be more likely to identify with you." Example: In 2009, during the Great Recession, Hyundai Motor America introduced a new "Warranty" program that allowed new car buyers and renters to obtain a new warranty. The company would buy its vehicle again up to one year after the original purchase, paying a maximum of $ 7,500 of the cost of depreciation, if the client lost his job within that period. Customers could also ask the company to take care of their car payments for 90 days while looking for work: without demanding money back. The announcement included television commercials and online advertisements with headlines such as "We support you for a full year" and "Guarantee: certainty in times of uncertainty." The result? Hyundai surpassed other automakers in February 2009. This campaign "distinguished them as a company that placed customers first, above their profits," Reibstein analyzes. As the economy began to recover in 2011, the program gradually withdrew.

Brands can also combine empathy with an understanding of customer behavior. When money is scarce, consumers tend to make certain predictable changes in their buying habits, for example they eat less. For food companies, that requires promotions and campaigns that not only take advantage of these behavioral patterns, but do so in solidarity. Another example: during the Great Recession of 2008, Kraft published more recipes with Kraft products on its website and on social networks. In a recession, people have less money and feel fear: brands can help relieve that anxiety; Psychology has discovered that a simple way to do this is to build a sense of control based on knowledge of the situation (just as Kraft did).

3 recipes

If you don't have time to read, take a look at cases like the following:

  1. During the 2008 recession Hyundai promised to assume part of the debt of a new car buyer if he was unemployed. Result: 10% more sales during 2009, compared to its competitors.
  2. In the same year Kraft developed and published hundreds of recipes based on the brand's economic products, with the aim of helping to lighten the anxiety of middle and lower class consumers
  3. Unliver maintained its spending on market research and advertising in the midst of this crisis, calculating that the ROI of what was spent would be even greater, because some competitors would stop investing in those items to save money.
