The success of disruptive advertising: women and #Showus

Carmen Zavala R.

In this article ❧ We tell how the advertising of sanitary pads changed (from blue blood to red), and explains that this change was based on insights related to consumer shame, guilt and taboo

Years ago I teach anthropology at a medicine school. Social science concepts are difficult for students, so I tried to illustrate them with everyday situations; Advertising and market research provide excellent inputs for this purpose. On one occasion I took to class “The Story of Menstruation”, an animated short that Walt Disney produced under the auspices of Kotex in 1946. The result was not what I expected, but I am sure of something: my students were not bored with that class; and they will no longer see Kotex advertising with the same eyes.

Kotex takes a step forward

The original goal of the short film was to expose a normal physiological process to a teenage audience in a fairly conservative country at the time. It was not obvious that the short was also a form of soft selling: Kotex financed the short to sell menstrual pads without mention menstrual pads. This is demonstrated by the brochure that accompanied the video ( “Very Personally Yours” ) that directly promoted Kotex products.

My students' reactions were mixed. Some viewed the video with sympathy for presenting useful and “fact-based” information; others expressed annoyance at a speech that forced women to put on their "prettiest face," even in "those days." Only one thing went unnoticed: the blood of the period was blue. The advertising of sanitary pads and tampons has repeatedly shown us this image: Blood that is not blood. Today, 74 years after that Walt Disney short, television commercials offer increasingly thinner and ergonomic products with greater ability to absorb ... a blue liquid.

It is not uncommon for menstrual flow to be simulated in this way, as menstruation has historically been associated with infertility, impurity, or anger. (Which woman has not been told that "she is in her days" when she is angry).

The "blue blood" of menstruation - which has nothing to do with royalty - has become a visual code. It has become so obvious that menstruation "is blue," that none of my medical students noticed. Eventually, I said to them myself, and the first thing they said was "Nosotras!" (the name the brand of Libresse has in Colombia) And then they remembered that the advertising of this brand dared to make visible what was obvious without being it: menstruation is red.

Bodyform (the English brand for Libresse) did a great campaign to break taboos and connect women globally. They found that everything related to the periods triggered shame, disgust and rejection. The insight was that brands that offered solutions for women's periods systematically avoided reality, thereby helping to perpetuate shame. In this situation, Bodyform assumed the responsibility of removing the stigmas of the periods. At the same time, they saw an opportunity to gain visibility, respect, and increased participation in a market dominated by Always and Tampax.

The creative idea was this: aggressive shock tactics or simple reporting would change nothing. "To assert the right of periods to be and feel normal, we had to be the normality we wanted to see." Hence #bloodnormal emerged. Bodyform ran a campaign on social networks and taking advantage of the rejection caused by menstruation, he did creative judo to gain visibility. Thus, it was sought to create a new paradigm of normality and positivity, in which women could stop feeling unpleasant and men disgusted.

The strategy worked. In the last three years, Bodyform sales in Europe decreased 0.5%, while P&G grew. But where #bloodnormal was promoted (Netherlands, Sweden, Norway, UK, France), Bodyform increased its market share by an average of 1.4%.

In Colombia, they launched the hashtag #ElRojoNoMeSonroja in order to create a space for dialogue on menstruation. In general, the decision to change the blue liquid for red, was applauded. Many Colombians expressed messages of relief on social networks.

Advertising becomes disruptive when it highlights uncomfortable or ignored realities. In this case the fact of the blood of the period is not unnoticed: the bold, the creative, the profitable, the disruptive thing was to show that the blood of the period is red.

Brands sell much more than a product. Their advertising shows us images and concepts that come to feel so real, that it seems that only advertising itself is capable of undoing them. This "reality" that builds advertising can become so normalized that it can also be a blind spot for advertisers and marketing managers. It is through judicious consumer research that insights can be discovered to illuminate those blind spots.

Advertising campaigns that show a more real point of view have caused a stir. An example is Billie and her #projectbodyhair. These feminine hair removal products dared to show women with hair. This marked a distance from the typical advertisements for waxing and shavers presented by hairless models. And it's not that we suddenly realized that women have hair, but that a blind spot was revealed. Presenting explicitly and real female body hair was what I liked the most. In this way, it was possible to show empathy. Consumers were able to identify with the protagonists of Billie's campaign, instead of feeling bad about not even looking like the “before” of the commercials.

Another brand that has opted for "real women" is Dove. All of them, regardless of size, age, skin color, hair texture, all have a "beauty potential" that Dove invites you to discover through its products. Without models, without photoshop, only flesh and blood women. But Dove wants to go further and take real women to another level. The #ShowUs project seeks to create, together with Getty Images and Girlgaze, a library of photographs of real women, made by them on their own terms. Thus, it seeks to break stereotypes and redefine beauty. With this bookstore, the media and advertisers have available a collection of photographs that pretends to be an authentic representation.

The #ShowUs project is characterized by:

  1. Being 100% powered by women both in front of and behind the scenes
  2. Point out diversity
  3. Represent real life and not a mere performance
  4. Avoid digital retouching
  5. Show self-defined beauty: each woman decides how she wants to be seen

Many have celebrated that these brands show a "more human" side of women: the new advertising fact seems to be that the more "imperfect" one is, the more beautiful it is. Perhaps having more achievable beauty models represents a relief for those who have been measured with the demanding bar of advertising for feminine products.

Disruptive advertising captures the charm of the real and manages to express it in its messages. Thus, brands not only sell their products, but also defy stereotypes that traditional advertising has spread, and help to show other ways of seeing and understanding the world. Brands become an inspiration rather than an aspiration.

Highlights on disruptive advertising:

  1. Advertising contributes to creating ideas, images and concepts about reality.
  2. The communication codes generated and / or promoted by advertising are not immutable.
  3. Advertising can be disruptive when "blind spots" are identified and brands propose a different way of looking at the world.
  4. Research is essential to generate insights capable of identifying those "blind spots"
