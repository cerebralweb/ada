UX Research for a Latin American bank

Emilio Talcott

In this article ❧ Itaú is the largest financial institution in Latin America. With more than 60 million customers, its profits exceed six billion of Reales per year. Its digital platform is like that: more of 100 UX designers work on the development of various applications.

This text is a translation of an excerpt from: Camila Borja and Luciano Lauri Pires, " UX Research in a Developing Country ", ESOMAR, Fusion, 2018.

The Brazilian context

Brazil is a profoundly unequal country and inequality occurs at all levels. For example, regarding ethnicity and color: among the people with the lowest income in the country, 78.5% are black or mestizo, while than 20.8% are white (source: Institute IBGE ofGeography and Statistics)In general, Brazilians have few or no positive references regarding the administration of money. According to a study carried out by Box1824 with more than 2,000 people, 97%of the sample agrees that they find it difficult to deal with their own money. In sum, the issue of money in banks is a taboo in Brazil, where 49% of respondents avoid even thinking about money to avoid feeling sad .

Digital banking

How to overcome mistrust in money and banking? Can a bank like Itaú help? With a large number of users having little financial knowledge, several challenges arise in the design process to create an user experience that meets the needs and desires of the people and that, at the sametime, is economically and technically feasible.

The UX Research has helped to understand the key elements to create and evolve our digital user experience. Those elements are today call the seven design principles, which guide our user-centered design process.

    7 design principles

  • Digital, from start to finish
  • Consistency to facilitate learning
  • Customer's time is valuable
  • Flawless, even when it fails
  • Speak the client's language
  • The client is in charge

However, these principles are not a guarantee of success, especially in a culture with negative perceptions of money and the bank itself. While the visual design and user experience are fundamental for people to feel safe on a digital platform, it is important to ensure a in-depth knowledge of the client through different research methods.

Lightbox app

Itaú is considered a high-class bank by many Brazilians. The history of a competitive bank and the way in which the brand has been communicated over the years have created an image of a powerful institution, with a great affinity with technology. This affinity is, indeed, an important part of the growth of the company. During the 1970s and 1980s, Itaú manufactured its own hardware for ATMs and personal computers . Today is the applications and websites that represent the company's technological vision.

By 2016, most customers were already banking through apps. It was the year where the company ventured into a plan to reach out to low-income potential customers, considering the omnipresence of smartphones in Brazil. Smartphones provided the opportunity to interact with this audience and, finally, converting more people into customers.

The Goal

The idea of the study was to know how to provide a simple and intuitive channel for low-income potential customers, in order to increase our customer base.

The challenge was taken up and led by an internal team of UX designers, sponsored by the area commercial in charge of digital channels. The extended team included people from marketing, visual design, customer services customer and IT.

The hypothesis

People with low incomes may be more reluctant and suspicious about the use of Itaú digital channels for banking. The reluctance can stem from unfamiliarity with technology and lack of affinity with brand. Suspicion may stem from fear of being robbed, lack of security and privacy issues; feeling that might be related to serious security problems in Brazil.

The method

A Design Thinking approach was used to guide the project through five main phases: exploration, synthesis, ideation, prototyping and testing.

In the exploration phase, a series of in-depth interviews were conducted with more than 30 people in four geographic areas of Brazil. The UX researchers visited these people in their homes and passed about two hours with them, talking about their personal lives, their needs and their relationship with money and technology. A large amount of information, in the form of annotations, photos, videos, and audio recordings, was analyzed in the synthesis phase.

The insights

Selling and integrating: challenges of digital banking

Some crucial observations emerge here.

  1. Low-income people in Brazil have very busy lives. They must work hard and, most of the time, they cannot save money. Many have multiple jobs that contribute to a stressful routine of commuting and long waits on public transport. Although this seems to be a perfect setting for a digital banking solution (one that could help them solve problems without having to go to a physical branch), the research showed they had a very little interest in it . Users had already included occasional visits to the banks premises in their routine, or they could trust someone to help them with payments and transfers.
  2. Translated to the business of digital banking, the insight tells us that to massively use digital banking in low and low-middle segments, patient and creative financial education work is necessary
  3. Apps in this context must be hybrid (run on all interfaces and operating systems), responsive (all kinds of screens), simple (minimalist) and consume little data and battery; otherwise, people will see them as another economic burden and will erase them.
  4. People were considered to have low digital self-esteem; there was little or no familiarity with computers and smartphones to access banks through apps or websites. Sometimes, the family alone had just one device so family members should share it. In these cases, younger family members were the most active users. Illiteracy is a common problem in this segment. Many have little or no ability to write and read correctly, limiting their willingness to use interactive solutions based on written instructions, as long as reasoning and decision-making activities.
  5. Communication tools and social networks were the most relevant applications for this segment. WhatsApp is by far the most visited application in Brazil: from the point of view of the user experience the main reason is its voice communication technology.
  6. A wide variety of devices was found, from simple phones to smart devices: two important restrictions would affect any initiative of a digital channel in this audience: limited storage space and limited data plans to access the internet.
