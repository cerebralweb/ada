Fancy Coffee

Tostao is turning the traditional coffee date in Bogotá upside down. In your premises you can check that some of the dominant brands' clientele is gone, or at least experimenting with the new option of consumption. The Hexis research team set out to observe for several weeks a group of clerks, who are regular costumers of the cafes. In that fieldwork we were able to verify that the choice of a lower priced product does not necessarily explained because of the size of the wallet. The challenge is to understand why a particular segment is been seduced, when not long ago they preferred to go to Oma or Juan Valdez . Did the demand change? Has the costmer budget been restructured? Was the effectiveness of the Tostao's Framing what caused such movement?

Bogotano coffee

There was a time when the cityzens of Bogotá did not drink coffee but chicha. Coffee was a drink of extravagant people who met to talk about smart things, politics, business. When the beer replaced chicha among the working classes, the small Bogota middle class preferred the distinction of "going to the cafe". People who had gone to college drank coffee, not beer. (Actually they drank both things: in the end, coffee had an air of sophistication that the beer compensated with relaxation, and noise).

There was another time - a long one - when each social group was adopting its own style of going to the cafe. The wealthy people drank coffee in a club, or in a place sufficiently expensive or remote; while the others used to take it in "the cafeteria": at that time both the worker, the janitor, the usher, the secretary as the businessman they took "un perico" or "un tinto", but in different ways and in different places different , of course it was.

And there was another time - more recent - when brands like Oma and Juan Valdéz appeared. Their model business was to create a local version of Starbucks, one that interpreted the famous Colombian coffee as a product who could also be sold in the cities of the country that produced it, and not only in Europe and the United States. And there it changed again the way of understanding what "going to the cafe" was: it was not only about entrepreneurs with a new idea, but also new middle classes growing and demanding new forms of consumption.

And now we are in another era, when the market turned upside down again. It is a bit like the revenge of the chicha. The reader will remember that three paragraphs ago it was said that chicha was replaced by beer. The businessmen simply decided that an industrial drink should replace an artisanal one, which hardly they manufactured. Although in those years there was no talk on marketing, marketing consisted of arguing that the chicha - a cheap fermented drink that was taken in improvised containers and in quantities not controlled-- it wasn't hygienic nor typical of civilized nations and cities, as was the beer, which came out ordered in bottles - the contents of which were measured and labeled -, mathematically arranged in boxes, and distributed by properly identified trucks, that carried quantities of boxes determined by what would be called "a distribution chain". Well , Tostao is like a business chica. The following section explains why.

A Blue Ocean

Tostao is to put the experience of "going to the cafe" within the scope of low income clerks, and enhance it with a cheap bakery portfolio.

So that, the specific value propositions in Tostao are:

  • Make a hybrid between the neighborhood bakery and the Starbucks coffee.
  • Contrary to appearances, Tostao's model is not based exclusively on the law of demand (the lower the price the more the buyers); It is also based on a very fashionable marketing framing: the democratization Of something.
  • Tostao joins brands that want to "democratize" something, in their case coffee (and now wine).
  • A highly diversified product portfolio.
  • If a store sells a lot, they open another one nearby.
  • By the end of 2019 Tostao will have more than 400 stores, and the economic stagnation will bring more clientele from ""middle" income segments
  • They only expand to cities with a mass market.

The Framing of Tostao

The consumer buys the best he can. You just need to clarify what it means to be able to buy something and how to define what is "the best" in each purchasing context.

First of all: Does Tostao sell cheap coffee? No, Tostao sells coffee cheaper than Oma, Juan Valdéz and Starbuks, but also sells coffee as expensive - sometimes more expensive - than many bakeries, cigarette shops and street sales; in fact a Sinnetic's recent report stated that Tostao's impact may be felt more in the informal economy. (Here el report .)

When prices and budgets change the consumption of goods also change . Tostao has contributed with more than 1,500 employees and 300 stores to change the price of "going out for coffee." At the same time, the economy Colombian is in a period of recession, VAT, fuels went up, that is, there has certainly been an adjustment in the budgets of families, especially the low and middle income segments (where the framing of Tostao).

In summary: playing with price and tight budgets is at the heart of the strategy of Tostao .

Being able to buy a coffee could mean saving money. Consumers are not always aware of being saving or wasting money. Perhaps what they feel ends up to be more important. Tostao is clear on this: the austerity of its facilities (there are no chairs or tables, for example) immediately makes you feel like you are there to save. However, when comparing their prices with those of the informal bakeries, savings become relative.

The coffee buyer must feel that he saves in these times, but at the same time he needs to feel that is acquiring enough value. Tostao has an answer to this expectation: standardization, uniformity, or, in in other words, a branding, a brand construction, which is a soft version of Oma, Juan Valdéz or Starbucks, and a much more robust version of artisan branding (didn't we just invent it?) of bakeries and cigar shops. neighborhood.

What is Framing ?

How things are presented to consumers, that is the "Framing". Brands must --in theory-- to sell what people want to buy. So marketing and advertising are like additional layers companies put on products to convince buyers, although the contents. Advertising and marketing do not necessarily increase the intrinsic value of something: not because the commercial from Old Spice is funny (my cousin laughs over and over at the same spot) his deodorant is going to to get better.

Incidentally, consumers are more likely to be influenced by marketing and advertising when their choices are constrained by uncertainty: uncertainty is so obvious in our local environment and worldwide.

Ethnography: 4 Tostao fans

It is said that if a consumer hesitates when buying something, it is because he is in a process of discovery: "Could it be that I do need this? "Will I buy it again?" "What do I feel when I'm buying it?". It happens with a tomato, a coffee, a car and even with the Mayor (when you go to vote you are making a consumption decision).

On the 24th, at seven thirty in the morning, Paulo - our ethnographer - took the bus that would lead him to the North Station. Then he took the route D10, sat in a blue chair (neither elderly nor pregnant women appeared), and after a few stops descended at the Virrey stop. Not far from there he met the marketing director of a supply firm for restaurants. What the company wanted to know has nothing to do with this report. The thing is that the commission involved do field work in various cafes in the city, including Tostao.

One day Paulo was buying his own coffee in a Tostao on the "Carrera 24" and "Calle 63", when a scary laugh made him turn. It was a woman of about 55 years who was drinking a coffee with three other clients. Since he had been there several times there he realize that it was the same group that he had seen before. The most striking thing, however, was that he had also seen them in an Oma premise nearby.

Without hesitation, Paulo decided to approach to the group. After introducing himself, he made conversation with them about the. ethnography, the study of the consumer and coffee, and proposed to interview them. He didn't even have to. invite them the next coffee.

"With what he bought me an OMA coffee he bought me a much bigger coffee and a bread or a cookie"

It turned out that all four were employees of a Jesuit school that was also in the area. The woman of the scary laugh was an accountant and had no family other than her mother (she lived with the first in Cedritos). Their pals were two teachers and the "Discipline Coordinator". One was the teacher of Ethics, religion, lived in Chía and she was married with a soccer player retired early due to a skull injury; they have a daughter (which she rarely saw awake, as much of her life is spent in the traffic between Bogotá and Chía). The other teacher is a non-practicing engineer who dictates biology and lives in an old apartment in La soledad with its wife (childless). The "Discipline Coordinator", although chatty, did not comment much about himself, but it was obvious that is about to retire.

After the first conversation, Paulo and the group of happy diners agreed to continue meeting in the following weeks to talk about coffee and its tastes. Some of the issues discussed were why did they switch from Oma to Tostao? What did they feel it was ease? Will the change be permanent? What could Oma do to get them back? What could Tostao do to retain them?

Insight

Tostao is to put the experience of "going to the cafe" within the reach of low and "middle" income, and add a bakery portfolio. Their main challenge is to selectively improve their facilities so that he Experimental consumption of mid clerks is transformed into a constant consumption.

To drink coffee is to put something in your stomach, but without "gain weigh"; drinking coffee is not eating something, it is a experience of sociability, relaxation and energy acquisition for a hard day at work.
