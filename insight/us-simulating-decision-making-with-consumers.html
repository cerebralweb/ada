New Solutions to Robot Responding

We can define "robot responding" as repeated profesional responding. it happens when a person regularly responds surveys as "panel" member, to obtain rewards. In the best scenario, that person really tries to respond "the true": the problem is that his mindset is too acquainted with survey methodology, so he almost knows what to respond just to get his reward. The consequence is huge amounts of data whose quality is compromised (maybe that's why they are cheap).

While the traditional (and beloved) surveys lead to professional respondents rather than the true target audience, some has been to rethink research through simulations of real world situations.

Doctors are a Wanted Target

Task Inc, for example, has developed a research approach for their target audience of physicians based not on asking them questions but on observing what they do in simulated scenarios.

Doctors, in fact, are a good example of people who is not willing to complete a survey; they're trained to solve puzzles, and are able to perform a complex simulation activity.

How does it work

What we do --explains Greg Chu, who conducted one of this researchs for Task inc.-- is create a queue of virtual or simulated patients, the physician can examine them [and] make treatment decisions. Then, based on algorithms, the status of those patients updates and they return in virtual time for a follow-up visit. Given the possibilities offered by technology such as VR, it's still a relatively crude exercise, ---Chu admitted--- There wasn't a lot of negative comment, he said; in fact, what was really interesting was how often the word 'fun' came up.

A particular value for researchers, is that, unlike traditional methods which might involve some rationalisation, this is more like observing the target in his natural environment, so to speak.

For example, two Alzheimer's patients can be given differing levels of severity in order to see how doctors treat them over time. "This is very important data that is very expensive to get hold of," Chu add. And being able to run the exercise in different markets also revealed how US doctors will treat the disease aggressively from the start, while Japanese doctors will "keep something in reserve as the patient declines".

How could it work better ?

The problem is there: massive and low quality data, reluctance to answer large sets of questions, especially when those questions are repetitive or just boring. The key is the interactivity. Participating in a marketing researching should not be fun; but it should be dinamic.

Simulate escenarios is a good idea. But you don't necessarily need virtual reality. Let's see:

    Effective forms of simulate consumer experience

  • Verbal story telling
  • Use of graphic assets
  • Simple (but seamless) animations

Findings

As always, some crucial observations arise here.

  1. Surveys are useful, only when applied with scientific standards: the problem is that that is expensive
  2. Simulation and gamification (especially the first) can help to introduce more dinamics in marketing research
  3. Simulation can be done in simple ways
  4. These is particularly useful with high-income-busy segments
